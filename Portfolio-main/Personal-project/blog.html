Shameless

28/12/24







When I first started watching Shameless, I wasn’t sure what to expect, but I quickly got hooked. The chaotic lives of the Gallagher family immediately pulled me in. It wasn’t just a wild ride; it was an emotional journey that made me care deeply about the characters and their messy lives. From the first episode, I knew I was in for something special.

What really impressed me was the complex characters. Frank Gallagher, despite being a terrible father, was oddly fascinating because of his moments of vulnerability. Fiona, meanwhile, held the family together, and her struggle to balance her own life while raising her siblings was both inspiring and heartbreaking. The way each character was developed made the show so much more than just a drama about a dysfunctional family.

I also loved how the show mixed drama with humour. The Gallaghers’ misfortunes often led to hilarious situations, and the comic timing was brilliant. Carl’s wild schemes and Lip’s sarcastic remarks had me laughing out loud, even in the midst of the chaos. It was this balance of light and dark that made the show so entertaining and unpredictable.

What made Shameless even more impactful was how it tackled real issues. From poverty and addiction to mental health and LGBTQ+ struggles, the show didn’t shy away from difficult topics. Ian’s journey, especially his struggles with bipolar disorder, stood out to me as both moving and eye-opening. The show’s willingness to confront these realities head-on made it feel genuine and relevant.

By the time I finished the last season, I realised how much I’d grown attached to the Gallaghers. Despite all their dysfunction, the sense of love and loyalty within the family was what made the show so compelling. Shameless wasn’t just wild and outrageous; it was a heartfelt exploration of resilience and family, and it left a lasting impression on me.

The Bear

03/01/25

When I started watching The Bear, I wasn’t sure I’d enjoy a show about a fast-paced sandwich shop. But from the first episode, I was hooked. It wasn’t just about food—it was a gripping, emotional story that had me invested in the characters and their struggles, revealing deeper themes of growth and redemption.

Carmy stood out as a complex character, torn between his ambition and personal demons, while the supporting cast, like Sydney and Richie, each had their own rich backstories. Their journeys made every episode more engaging and real.

I also loved how the show balanced intense drama with lighter, humorous moments. The tension in the kitchen was always high, but the humour kept things from feeling too heavy, making it a perfect mix of fast-paced action and wit.

What really hit home was how The Bear portrayed the characters’ emotional and professional challenges. Carmy’s mental health struggles and the pressure of running the business were shown in such an authentic way that I could easily relate, even without experience in the industry.

By the end of the season, I was deeply attached to the characters. Despite their flaws, the team’s bond through shared hardship made the show more than just about food. The Bear was a touching exploration of ambition, trauma, and healing.
